  <div style="font-size: 1.3em; font-weight: bold; margin-top: 0.2em; margin-bottom: 0.5em;">
    Gliding in the Amazonian canopy: adaptive evolution of flight in <em>Morpho</em> butterflies
  </div>

  <span style="color:#D0D0D0; font-size: 0.9em">
    Understanding how colonization of a novel habitat drives the evolution of animal traits is a central question in evolutionary biology. In the canopy of the Amazon rainforest, the slow — and seemingly peaceful — gliding flight of some <em>Morpho</em> species appears to be a specialized adaptation among butterflies.
  </span>

  <div style="display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 1.5em; margin-top: 1.5em">
    <span style="color:#D0D0D0; font-size: 0.9em; flex: 1;">
      We studied the divergence in both flight behavior and wing shape among <em>Morpho</em> butterflies living in different forest strata, by combining high-speed
      videography in the field with morphometric analyses and aerodynamic modelling. We showed that microhabitat specialization has resulted in the adaptive divergence of flight behaviour and wing shape, opposing efficient gliding flight in species living in the canopy to powerful flapping flight in species living in the understory. We further investigated for divergence in evasive climbing flight, revealing that canopy species also achieve higher climbing efficiency through changes in wingbeat kinematics and morphology. 
    </span>
    <img src="/assets/images/cisseis in canopy.gif" style="border-radius: 10px; width: 40%; object-fit: cover;">
  </div>

  <div style="font-size: 1.3em; font-weight: bold; margin-top: 0.2em; margin-bottom: 0.5em;">
    Unravelling the coexistence of mimetic <em>Morpho</em> butterfly species
  </div>

  <span style="color:#D0D0D0; font-size: 0.9em">
    <em>Morpho</em> blue signal reduces individual predation by advertising their high escape ability, favoring resemblance between species living in the same locality. This led to the widespread wing pattern convergence of several blue-banded <em>Morpho</em> butterfly species with overlapping ranges of distribution (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/jeb/article/34/2/284/7326676" style="color:#D0D0D0;" target="_blank">Llaurens <em>et al.,</em> 2021</a>). But does the convergence in wing color pattern among co-occuring <em>Morpho</em> species lead to heterospecific rivalry or courtship?
  </span>

  <div style="margin-top: 1.5em; margin-bottom: 1.5em; text-align: center;">
    <img src="/assets/images/13h04min45sec_05.08_combined.gif" alt=" " style="width: 75%; border-radius: 10px;">
  </div>

  <span style="color:#D0D0D0; font-size: 0.9em">
    Using solar-powered dummy butterflies placed in the wild, we show that this morphological convergence induces interspecific behavioral interference, by triggering erroneous courtship and male-male competition. Genomic data nevertheless revealed a surprising strong genetic isolation between species despite these inter-specific interactions.
  </span>

  <div style="display: flex; justify-content: center; gap: 2.5em; margin: 1.5em 0;">
    <img src="/assets/images/me catching Morpho.png" alt=" " style="width: 42%; border-radius: 10px;">
    <img src="/assets/images/patrolling time.png" alt=" " style="width: 42%; border-radius: 10px;">
  </div>

  <span style="color:#D0D0D0; font-size: 0.9em">
    We performed a mark-recapture experiment and discovered a remarkable temporal divergence in flight activity among species, probably facilitating their coexistence and explaining their isolation. Hence, the convergence in morphology and divergence in phenology likely favor species coexistence by partitioning niche in different dimensions.
  </span>
